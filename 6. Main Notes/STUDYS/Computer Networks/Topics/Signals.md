### **Signals**

#### **Definition**:

- A signal is a physical quantity that conveys information, typically transmitted over a medium such as a wire, fiber-optic cable, or wireless channel. Signals can be either analog (continuous) or digital (discrete), each having unique characteristics that influence their ability to convey data.

#### **Types of Signals**:

1. **Analog Signals**:
    
    - **Description**: Continuous signals that vary smoothly over time, representing an infinite range of values.
    - **Examples**: Sound waves, radio waves, electrical signals in analog devices.
    - **Characteristics**:
        - Continuous in nature, can represent all possible values within a given range.
        - Susceptible to distortion and attenuation over long distances.
    - **Use Case**: Analog telephones, AM and FM radio broadcasting.
2. **Digital Signals**:
    
    - **Description**: Discrete signals representing binary data (0s and 1s) encoded as pulses.
    - **Examples**: Computer data, digital television, modern communication systems.
    - **Characteristics**:
        - Represented as square waves, consisting of distinct high (1) and low (0) states.
        - Less prone to degradation from noise and interference compared to analog signals.
    - **Use Case**: Ethernet communication, Wi-Fi networks, fiber-optic data transmission.

#### **Characteristics of Signals**:

1. **Amplitude**:
    
    - **Definition**: The height or strength of a signal, usually measured in volts or decibels.
    - **Importance**: Higher amplitude signals are more likely to be detected accurately, but excessive amplitude can cause distortion.
    - **Impact of Low Amplitude**: Weak signals may not be detected, resulting in data loss or errors.
2. **Frequency**:
    
    - **Definition**: The number of cycles a signal completes per second, measured in Hertz (Hz).
    - **Importance**: Higher frequencies can carry more data and are often used for higher bandwidth applications.
    - **Impact of Low Frequency**: Lower frequencies offer longer range but carry less data, making them suitable for slower communication.
3. **Phase**:
    
    - **Definition**: The position of the signal's wave within its cycle, typically measured in degrees.
    - **Importance**: Phase is crucial for distinguishing multiple signals and accurately reconstructing data.
    - **Impact of Phase Shift**: A phase shift can cause data misinterpretation, leading to errors in communication.

#### **Signal Propagation**:

- **Description**: The way signals travel through different transmission media such as copper cables, fiber optics, or wireless channels.
- **Factors Affecting Propagation**:
    - **Attenuation**: The loss of signal strength as the signal travels through a medium, causing a reduction in signal quality.
    - **Noise**: External interference that distorts or alters the signal, leading to potential errors.
    - **Distortion**: Any change in the shape of the signal, typically caused by the transmission medium or other environmental factors.

#### **Signal to Noise Ratio (SNR)**:

- **Definition**: The ratio of the strength of the signal to the level of background noise.
- **Importance**: A higher SNR means clearer signal transmission, while a lower SNR indicates a higher likelihood of errors due to noise.
- **Measurement**: Measured in decibels (dB); the higher the dB value, the clearer the signal.

#### **Signal Modulation**:

- **Definition**: The process of altering a carrier signal (usually a sinusoidal wave) to encode data for transmission.
    
- **Types of Modulation**:
    
    1. **Amplitude Modulation (AM)**:
        - **Description**: The amplitude of the carrier signal is varied in accordance with the data.
        - **Use Case**: AM radio broadcasting.
    2. **Frequency Modulation (FM)**:
        - **Description**: The frequency of the carrier signal is altered to represent data.
        - **Use Case**: FM radio, TV signals.
    3. **Phase Modulation (PM)**:
        - **Description**: The phase of the carrier signal is shifted to encode data.
        - **Use Case**: Digital communication systems like Wi-Fi.
